Handmade Web

J. R. Carpenter, March 2015

The term 'handmade' usually refers to objects made by hand or by using simple tools rather than machines.

The result may be homely — or exquisite.

As in a child's clay ashtray — or a pair of bespoke brogues.

I evoke the term 'handmade web' to refer to web pages coded by hand rather than by software.

Web pages made and maintained by individuals rather than by businesses or corporations.

Provisional. Temporary. One-of-a-kind.

Web pages which challenge conventions of reading, writing, design, ownership, privacy, security, or identity.

Handmade web pages flourished in the mid-to-late-1990s.

After the academic web. Before the corporate web.

‘Handmade’ is by no means the only or best term to define the web of this period.

In her essay, A Vernacular Web (2005), Olia Lialina describes the web of the mid-1990s as:

bright, rich, personal, slow and under construction.

It was a web of sudden connections and personal links.

Pages were built on the edge of tomorrow.

Full of hope for a faster connection and a more powerful computer.

It was a web of amateurs soon to be washed away by dot.com ambitions.

Professional authoring tools. Guidelines. Usability experts.

For more information, see: One Terabyte of Kilobyte Age.

I evoke the term 'handmade web' in order to make a correlation between handmade web pages and handmade print materials.

Zines. Pamphlets. Artists books.

I made my first web-based work in 1995.

The impetus came from fine art and book-making practices.

The text evolved from an installation exhibition.

I created a small book-work meant to tell a circular story.

But books end. People stop reading.

In the web version, the last page linked to the first.

The story circled round and round.

No physical evidence remains of the installation.

Only one copy of the book-work exists.

The handmade website still works.

It persists.

The handmade web emerged when print and digital were symbiotic.

This is evident in trAce Online Writing Centre.

One of the most influential online writing communities.

The archive reflects the context of its creation.

Unlike museum artifacts created elsewhere.

It exists where it was made.

Viewing old web pages creates a temporal paradox.

Layers of aesthetics overlap like wallpaper.

Pages shrink. Errors appear. Images disappear.

Ghosts of browsers past.

Warnings from earlier eras.

These are not artifacts of a dead web.

They are signs of a living web.

I evoke the term 'handmade web' to advocate active engagement.

Owning identity through personal websites.

Millions now do not know they are using the internet.

The web grows louder. Bigger. More distant.

We long for smaller, quieter spaces.

DIY aesthetics return in communities like TILDE.CLUB.

Some revisit. Some leave pages blank.

Elsewhere, Web 1.0 never disappeared.

Out-of-date design persists in code.

Out-of-date is not obsolete.

I delay updating my templates.

Until outdated becomes cool again.

I evoke the term 'handmade web' to draw attention to labour.

The web page as manual. As instruction.

Readers once viewed source.

Copied. Pasted. Rewrote.

Readers became writers.

Unindexed existed until it was indexed — then disappeared.

Taroko Gorge rewritten again and again.

Generated text. Endless output.

I evoke the term 'handmade web' to draw attention to the body.

The hand scrolls.

The hand works.

The body measures the web.

Virtual space becomes physical distance.

Handheld devices distance us from making.

The iPad hides how things work.

No source. No rewriting.

Consumption replaces making.

I evoke the term 'handmade web' to suggest slowness and smallness.

As resistance.

The more proprietary the web becomes,

the more committed I am to using it in poetic and intransigent ways.
