The Handmade Web

mid-to-late 1990s internet culture

The term handmade usually refers to objects made by hand or by using simple tools rather than machines. The result may be homely — as in a child's clay ashtray — or exquisite — as in a pair of bespoke brogues.

I evoke the term 'handmade web' to refer to web pages coded by hand rather than by software; web pages made and maintained by individuals rather than by businesses or corporations; web pages which are provisional, temporary, or one-of-a-kind.

Handmade web pages flourished in the mid-to-late-1990s, in the brief period after the academic web and before the corporate web.

“bright, rich, personal, slow and under construction… pages were built on the edge of tomorrow…”
— Olia Lialina, A Vernacular Web (2005)

The handmade web emerged at a time when print and digital enjoyed a symbiotic relationship. Early online writing communities such as trAce reflected this hybrid culture of zines, photocopies, and hypertext.

Viewing old web pages today produces a temporal paradox. Layer upon layer of dated aesthetics overlap like wallpaper. Pages optimised for lower resolutions occupy only a portion of the screen. Ghosts of browsers past mingle with missing images and dead links.

These are not artifacts of a dead web but signposts on a map of a living web — a web in progress, a web in the making.

In the early social web, there was a broad expectation that regular people might own their identities by having their own websites rather than relying on large platforms.

Nostalgia for the DIY intimacy of the early web continues today. Communities such as TILDE.CLUB revisit amateur web aesthetics, while others simply occupy the territory.

For most of its history, readers could view page source, copy code, and rewrite it. In this way, readers became writers.

Hand-coding and self-publishing experimental web art remains a radical act within today’s commercialised web environment.

The more proprietary and predatory the web becomes, the more committed I am to using it in poetic and intransigent ways.

J. R. Carpenter, March 2015
